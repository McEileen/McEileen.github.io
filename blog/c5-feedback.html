Feedback

More Than Just a Janet Jackson Song

August 9, 2015

It’s hard for me to make any sort of general or summarizing statement about the experience of pair-programming because it varies so much session by session. Sometimes pair programming takes far less time than expected and we both end it in a happy place. Other times my pair and I have gotten stuck and agreed to finish it on our own afterward. I find it particularly fun and rewarding when my pair and I get to teach each other new tricks and techniques. I always make a note of it when my pair explains a new method to me. I find it very difficult when I feel that I am not contributing to the session or holding up my end of the work. When I really struggle with this tendency, I find that the amount of time I spend worrying about my performance actually distracts me from programming and distances me from my pair. I am trying to work on this by looking up new techniques more proactively and letting my pair know when I am confused.

At first I felt antsy when I read my feedback, I think because I read all of it at once. I am now much more used to it and I find the process of reading feedback incredibly helpful. One thing that I find intriguing is how sometimes pairs who offer constructive feedback hedge a little bit and give a disclaimer beforehand about how they really enjoyed working with me, etc., but they have to give constructive feedback so (*insert feedback here*). The disclaimers make me wonder if the pair is worried about hurting my feelings or offending me. Often, their feedback is incredibly helpful and I am very glad to hear it. No need for the disclaimer!

Based on the feedback that I received, I am going to spend more time studying concepts and general implementations of Ruby concepts. One of my pairs noted that I used examples a lot when I was pairing, and I sometimes wasn’t flexible in how I approached the process of using methods. I appreciated this and will definitely be brushing up on my Ruby concepts.

When I first wrote feedback, I wrote all of it at once and a few days after my pair sessions. This proved challenging because I did not remember the sessions as well as I would have liked. I shifted to writing feedback the day of or the day after pairing. It’s much easier to give feedback this way because I remember our session more clearly. It initially proved difficult for me to write feedback because I had overly high standards and worried that my feedback wouldn’t be helpful or thorough enough for my partners. After reading feedback I had received, I realized that I often got great benefit from just one sentence in the feedback. This helped me to lower my standards and approach the art of giving feedback in a more calm manner.

Overall, I have a positive opinion of the process of using pairing and feedback to guide my learning. I generally seek out feedback after a college internship when I had a negative experience due to a lack of feedback. My supervisor and I did not communicate about expectations, and I consequently did not perform as either of us had desired. Though this is a learning experience rather than a job, I still remain committed to getting feedback.
